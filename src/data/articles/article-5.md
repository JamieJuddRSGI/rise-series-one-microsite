### The Billing Model Contradiction  

The research data reveals a fascinating three-way contradiction about how the Indian legal market prices its work:  

**Perspective 1 - The Client View**: Clients describe a shift toward value-based discussions and outcome-linked models, with flexibility and transparency as priorities.  

**Perspective 2 - The Firm View**: The market operates primarily on lump-sum mandates at compressed rates, with true hourly billing rare despite what firms claim.  

**Perspective 3 - The Self-Reported Data**: When 56 lawyers were asked what percentage of their private capital billings are charged on an hourly rate, the responses reveal a deeply divided market:  

![While most still usually charge by the hour, a sizable minority buck that trend.](IMG001.png)

The pricing paradox becomes clearer when examining how lawyers bill their private capital work. When 56 lawyers were surveyed about what percentage of their billings are charged on an hourly rate, responses ranged from 0% to 100%, revealing not a market in transition, but a market polarised.  

Nearly half (48%) report that 75-100% of their work is billed hourly, while a quarter (25%) report that less than 25% is hourly. The middle ground, firms billing 25-75% hourly, accounts for only 34% of respondents. This suggests the market has already divided into distinct segments, likely driven by client origin (foreign vs domestic) and engagement structure (direct vs through portfolio companies).  

One firm captures this split perfectly: *"India has always been AFAs. Fixed fees. It's actually only recently been getting clients to think about per hour. Foreign clients I can bill by the hour. If you're working domestically, it's definitely fixed fees."*

> ### What this distribution suggests:  
>
> - The market isn't transitioning uniformly from one model to another; it's operating as multiple parallel markets  
> - The "predominantly hourly" firms (75-100%) likely serve international clients directly or are positioned at the premium end  
> - The "predominantly non-hourly" firms (0-50%) likely serve domestic clients or work through portfolio companies  
> - The small middle suggests firms must commit to either premium positioning or volume efficiency; the middle ground is genuinely squeezed  

This explains why different perspectives on pricing can all feel accurate: they're describing different segments of the same market, operating under fundamentally different economic models.  

### The Blended Rate Reality  

Buried in the data is perhaps the real story: blended rates. One firm reports 65% of billing on blended rates, with only 15% pure hourly and 20% fixed. Several others cite blended rates as a significant portion of their work.  

Blended rates represent a middle ground—not the pure hourly billing, nor the fixed fees that some firms claim as standard, but a hybrid that gives the appearance of hourly billing while functioning more like capped or estimated arrangements.  

>**What this suggests**: When firms say, "hourly billing," they often mean something more nuanced—blended rates, estimates with assumptions, or hourly with caps. The terminology obscures the reality, and the same arrangements might be described differently by different firms.  

### The Estimation Paradox  

The majority (80-92.5%) of firms claim to use processes, tools, or analytics to produce better fee estimates. Yet when pressed, many describe their approach as: *"We use past data to arrive at fixed fee numbers; "* or *"We typically arrive at fee estimates based on our experience of having dealt with similar transactions in the past."* 

Only one firm mentions a sophisticated tool: a *"Matter Margin Billing tool that leverages historical deal insights to provide consistent and informed fee estimates for repeat or similar matters."* 

**What this suggests**: There's a significant gap between what firms claim as "data-driven estimation" (which is often just institutional memory) and actual analytical tools. Most firms are using experience and judgement, then labelling it as a process.  

### The Value-Add Gap  

Despite sophisticated pricing discussions, most firms score lowly on value-adds beyond efficient execution. Even firms offering alternative fee arrangements struggle to articulate what additional value justifies their pricing beyond "senior attention" and "reliable delivery."  

The handful of firms scoring highly on value-adds mention:  

- Client dashboards and portals  
- Regular newsletters, webinars, and trend reports  
- Curated regulatory updates and automated reminders  
- Market benchmarking using aggregated deal experience  
- Training and knowledge transfer  
- Mid-deal feedback loops  

> **What this suggests:** Pricing innovation alone doesn't differentiate. Clients are willing to pay for value, but firms need to define and deliver that value in tangible, visible ways beyond the core legal work.

### Innovation in Fees

While the hourly vs fixed rate debate dominates, our data reveals several pricing innovations that firms are actually implementing:  

- **Milestone-based payments**: Fixed fees with payments at transaction close, aligning firm risk with client outcomes  
- **Abort fees**: Risk-sharing mechanisms for deals that don't complete  
- **Activity-based pricing**: Pricing based on specific deliverables rather than time  
- **Caps and long-stop dates**: Providing certainty while maintaining hourly mechanics  
- **Transparent timesheets**: Sharing detailed breakdowns even on fixed-fee work  

> **What this suggests**: Innovation is happening incrementally within existing structures rather than through wholesale abandonment of hourly billing.  

### The Foreign vs Domestic Split  

Multiple data points reveal a clear divide in pricing approach based on client origin and engagement structure:  

- **Foreign clients engaging directly**: More accepting of hourly rates, value discussions, and premium pricing  
- **Domestic clients or foreign clients engaging through Indian entities**: Strong pressure for rupee-based pricing, fixed fees, and rate compression  

One firm notes: *"Even private capital firms increasingly engage through the local company, and then the conversation goes to 'why don't you do rupee rates?'"*

> **What this suggests:** The structure of the engagement matters as much as the client relationship. Even sophisticated international investors behave differently when procurement runs through their Indian portfolio companies. This divide likely explains the distribution in the billing data—different client types drive firms toward opposite ends of the billing spectrum.
